# Fashion Knowledge

The knowledge base for the fashion specialist. It covers the design pipeline, frameworks, materials, craft techniques, cultural heritage, tools, sourcing, segments, and presentation. It treats culture as origin, meaning, and community, never as decoration. Tier-zero seeded knowledge, refresh source facts only when verifying a specific detail. No em dashes, ever.

## How fashion design actually works

Every task maps to a stage. The specialist can enter at any stage.

| Stage | What happens | What it produces |
|---|---|---|
| Research and concept | study audience, season, cultural reference, market gap, trend, and the story | a concept with audience, mood, story, and references |
| Moodboard | gather color, texture, silhouette, references, swatches, motifs | a moodboard (generate via Higgsfield or Figma, or describe for physical assembly) |
| Sketching and silhouette | define shapes, proportions, key pieces | flat sketches, silhouette studies, a lineup |
| Fabric and material | choose textile for design, climate, culture, price | a materials list with weight, drape, source, cost |
| Pattern and construction | translate sketch to pattern, seams, closures, fit | construction notes, pattern guidance, technical flats |
| Sampling and prototyping | first physical piece, tested for fit and movement | a sample review checklist and fit notes |
| Production | scale to batch, sizing, grading, quality, supply chain | production specs, size grading, a quality checklist |
| Presentation | lookbook, campaign, runway, or direct-to-consumer | lookbook direction, styling notes, content briefs |

## The design frameworks

- **The 3-3-3 rule.** For a focused capsule, limit to three silhouettes, three fabrics, and three colors. It forces clarity and prevents overdesign. Use it when a user starts broad.
- **Silhouette first.** The silhouette is the identity, visible before color, detail, or fabric. Define it early: fitted, relaxed, structured, draped, voluminous, minimal. Proportion and movement matter more than surface detail.
- **Fabric dictates behavior.** Weight, drape, stretch, opacity, and hand decide what a garment can do. Always name the fabric. A bias cut in silk behaves nothing like a bias cut in cotton.
- **Moodboard as a decision tool.** A filter, not a pile of pretty images. It answers: what is the mood, who is the person, what world does this live in, what does it exclude. Build with a hero image at 30 to 40 percent, supporting references, a palette of four to seven colors, and white space.
- **Concept before collection.** "Nice clothes" is not a concept. "Dhaka patterns reimagined for Kathmandu streetwear, making heritage wearable for college students" is a concept.

## Nepal's textile heritage

Treat with respect: know origin, technique, community, and meaning.

- **Dhaka.** The signature Nepali textile. Handwoven on wooden handlooms using a supplementary weft technique, the weaver inserts colored patterns by hand into the base. Originally from the Limbu people of eastern Nepal (Terhathum), later refined by the Newa community. Traditional colors black, white, red, orange, with geometric motifs (diamonds, stars, zigzags) carrying meaning (a diamond for a peak, a zigzag for a river). No two pieces identical. Centers in Palpa (Tansen) and eastern Nepal. About half a meter per weaver per day. Modern Dhaka blends with silk, explores new colors, and appears in streetwear and accessories, not just topis. The Dhaka topi is essential to men's formal dress. Gunyo cholo uses Dhaka for the coming-of-age ceremony.
- **Pashmina.** From the undercoat of the Chyangra (Changthangi) goat at high altitude, hand-spun and woven on frame looms. Distinct from Kashmiri Pashmina in technique and natural dyeing. Authentic Pashmina is lightweight, warm, and lasts generations. Warn against fraud: most "handmade" Pashmina is 70 to 80 percent machine-spun. True handmade is documented goat to garment.
- **Allo (Himalayan nettle).** Wild-harvested giant nettle fiber, ancestral processing. Strong, textured, sustainable. Used for jackets, bags, accessories. Autumn harvest, winter processing, hand weaving.
- **Traditional garments.** Daura suruwal (men's national dress), gunyo cholo (women's set), chaubandi cholo (blouse), khasto (shawl), Haku Patasi (Newari black sari with red border), Chuba (Tibetan-influenced robe). Each carries cultural, regional, and ceremonial meaning.
- **Weaving communities and fair trade.** Over 91 percent of weavers in Nepal's artisan textile sector are women. Mahaguthi (since 1984), Dhaka Weaves (since 1991), and STEP-certified cooperatives support fair wages and women's independence. Always consider the human supply chain: who made this, are they paid fairly, is the craft sustained.
- **The cultural rule.** Traditional textiles, motifs, and garments carry meaning that belongs to their communities. Use them with understanding and attribution, never as surface decoration. When a design draws on Dhaka or Newari techniques, name the origin, explain the meaning, and consider whether the use honors or diminishes the tradition.

## Textile craft techniques

Real making processes, not just labels. Identify which applies and advise on materials, tools, and realistic expectations.

- **Weaving.** Interlacing warp and weft on a loom. Lap or rigid heddle loom (accessible, scarves, learning structure), floor loom (wider, faster, more pattern control), backstrap and pit looms across Nepal and South Asia. Loom type sets width, tension, and pattern complexity. Dhaka's supplementary weft is a specific technique, not a general term.
- **Embroidery.** Decorating existing fabric with needle and thread. Surface (stitches on top), counted-thread (on the weave grid, like cross-stitch), and whitework (white on white, like chikankari). Stitch families: running, satin, chain, stem, French knot, couching, long-and-short.
- **Cross-stitch.** A counted-thread X-stitch on an even-weave grid. Precise, geometric, accessible. Its own charting system and fabric (Aida or evenweave linen).
- **Needle felting.** Sculpting raw wool roving by stabbing it with a barbed needle until fibers tangle into a shaped form. Dry felting, no loom or thread. The barbs do the interlocking.
- **Macrame.** Knotting cords into patterns. Core knots: square and spiral (half-square). Structure through knots, not stitches. Cord thickness and material (cotton rope, jute, nylon) define the result.
- **Punch needle.** A hollow tool punches loops of yarn through a backing from behind, a plush textured front. Loops pushed through, unlike embroidery's pulled thread.
- **Nalbinding.** An ancient Scandinavian technique older than knitting. A single flat needle loops yarn through itself, each row a separate length. A dense, stretchy fabric that does not unravel if cut.
- **Knitting and crochet.** Knitting uses two needles and interlocking loops in rows. Crochet uses one hook pulling loops through loops. Both make stretch fabric from yarn. Advise on yarn weight, gauge, and fit.

## Global textile traditions

For understanding origin, technique, and meaning, never for stripping a look. These are living traditions with artisan communities.

### India

| Tradition | Region | Technique | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chikankari | Lucknow | white-on-white floral on muslin, cotton, or silk, around 30 stitches including jali (net) | subtle, refined, texture over color |
| Zardozi | Persian origin, Mughal India | gold and silver wire, beads, stones on silk or velvet, raised 3D work | opulent, regal, central to bridal couture |
| Kantha | West Bengal, Odisha | running-stitch quilting of old saris with folk patterns | recycled, storytelling, the original upcycled textile |
| Phulkari | Punjab | darning stitch from the reverse on hand-woven cotton with floss silk | bold, vibrant, geometric. Bagh covers nearly the whole cloth |
| Kasuti | Karnataka | counted-thread, no knots, reversible and identical both sides | precise, mathematical, technically demanding |
| Kutch | Gujarat | chain, buttonhole, darning, satin with mirrors (abhla) | colorful, sparkling, festive |
| Aari | Gujarat, Rajasthan | chain stitch with a hooked needle, sequins and beads | intricate, layered, bridal and festive |
| Kalamkari | Andhra Pradesh | hand-painted or block-printed narrative with natural dyes | painterly, narrative, fabric illustrated not stitched |

### China

Over 4,000 years of history, four great schools, each a National Intangible Cultural Heritage.

| School | Region | Defining character |
|---|---|---|
| Su (Su xiu) | Suzhou | the most refined, silk split to 1/128, double-sided embroidery on translucent silk, over 2,500 years, 40+ stitches |
| Xiang | Changsha, Hunan | the spirit of Chinese painting, realistic gradient portraits and animals |
| Shu | Chengdu, Sichuan | over 3,000 years, smooth and neat, 122 approaches across 12 categories |
| Yue (Canton) | Guangdong | vivid, dense, includes gold-thread (Chao), bold and decorative |

Also: the Forbidden Stitch (Peking knot), once reserved for the court, and the silk tradition including brocade (Yun, Song, Shu, Zhuang) and Kesi tapestry.

### Africa

| Tradition | Region | Technique | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kente | Ghana, Ashanti and Ewe | narrow-strip weaving in silk and cotton, strips sewn together | bold named geometric patterns, once royal |
| Adire | Nigeria, Yoruba | indigo resist (hand-painted, tie, stitch) | deep indigo and white, mythology and nature motifs |
| Bogolan (Mudcloth) | Mali, Bambara | hand-woven cotton dyed with fermented mud | earthy, spiritual, symbolic patterns |
| Ankara (Dutch wax) | West and Central Africa, made in NL, now also Ghana, Nigeria, China | wax-resist print on cotton | vibrant, named patterns carrying proverbs |
| Kanga and Kitenge | East Africa | printed cotton, often with Swahili proverbs | communicative, the text is part of the design |
| Shweshwe | South Africa, Sotho and Xhosa | indigo-dyed printed cotton | geometric, structured, heritage fabric |

Ankara's origin is complex: made in Europe for an Asian market, adopted across Africa, now a symbol of African identity despite non-African manufacturing. Handle with accuracy, not simplification.

### Training materials and testing

The specialist builds training sets (illustrated stitch diagrams, written instructions, practice patterns, common mistakes, material lists) tailored to skill level, and logs them as recipes in the vault. For cultural techniques, it adds context (origin, meaning, community), a simplified starter project, sourced materials, and an honest note on authenticity (what a learner can call their work versus what needs years of mastery). It helps test digitally before physical commitment: preview charts and color layouts, estimate yarn or thread, place embroidery on a flat in Illustrator or Figma, tile a print repeat, map warp and weft order, and test drape and fit in CLO3D or Blender.

## Efficient approaches to each technique

- **Weaving.** Start with a rigid heddle loom. Plan the warp before threading, calculate length with 10 percent waste. For Dhaka supplementary weft, learn the plain weave base first, then add the pattern weft pick by pick. Yarn weight and fiber decide everything: cotton for structure, silk for sheen, wool for warmth.
- **Embroidery.** Transfer the design first (water-soluble pen, iron-on, lightbox, printed stabilizer). Hoop taut. Learn five stitches first: running, backstitch, satin, stem, French knot. Use no more than 18 inches of thread at a time. Cultural traditions are not interchangeable, study the specific stitch set.
- **Cross-stitch.** Start with 14-count Aida and a counted pattern. Stitch all crosses the same direction. Use a charting tool to convert designs.
- **Needle felting.** Coarse roving and a multi-needle tool for shaping, then a single fine needle for detail. Core shape first, then surface. Foam pad underneath. Expect rough first pieces.
- **Macrame.** Learn the square knot and the lark's head. Measure cord at four times the finished length. Mount at eye level. Cotton rope 3mm to 5mm to start.
- **Punch needle.** Monk's cloth or tight linen backing. Flat needle, consistent depth. Design drawn on the back, appears reversed on the front.
- **Nalbinding.** Blunt flat needle, medium wool. York or Oslo stitch to start. Slower than knitting, denser and warmer.
- **Knitting and crochet.** Knitting: worsted yarn, 5mm needles, learn knit, purl, cast on, bind off. Crochet: 5mm hook, learn chain, single, double. Always gauge swatch first.

## Material sourcing

Never invent a source, price, or URL. Seeded knowledge first, targeted search for specific suppliers or current prices, Research Scout for market-wide scans. Flag anything unverified.

**Nepal (tier zero):** Dhaka from Palpa (Tansen), Terhathum, and Kathmandu outlets (Mahaguthi and Dhaka Weaves in Kupondole, Kalamandir), handloom only, verify provenance. Pashmina from highland cooperatives and Kathmandu showrooms, look for certified Chyangra Pashmina (CPI mark), beware fakes. Allo from Solu-Khumbu, Mustang, and eastern hills, seasonal autumn harvest, plan lead time. Cotton, silk, wool from Kathmandu wholesale (Ason, New Road), mostly imported from India with local weaving adding value.

**India (tier zero for known hubs):** Chikankari from Lucknow (Aminabad, Chowk), verify hand vs machine by the back. Zardozi and aari from Delhi (Nizamuddin), Lucknow, Bhopal, Surat, verify real metal zari. Kantha from Bolpur (Shantiniketan) and Kolkata, recycled sari vs fresh fabric differ. Phulkari from Patiala, Amritsar, Jaipur, verify hand vs machine. Block print from Jaipur (Sanganer, Bagru) and Ahmedabad, natural vs chemical dye matters.

**Global (tier light search when needed):** Ankara from Vlisco (original), GTP and Woodin (Ghana), Hitarget and Phoenix (budget Chinese), verify the tier. Kente from Bonwire (Ghana) for authentic handwoven. Chinese silk and embroidery from Suzhou, Chengdu, Hangzhou, Guangzhou, check stitch density and the back. Wholesale via Alibaba, IndiaMART, TradeNepal, never bulk-buy without a sample. Sustainable via GOTS, OEKO-TEX, and Fair Trade certified, ask for documentation not just claims.

## Segments

| Segment | What they need | How to think about it |
|---|---|---|
| Streetwear and daily wear | wearable, affordable, expressive, youth-driven | concept-led, silhouette-forward, references worn casually, social-first |
| Bridal and ceremonial | high craft, cultural accuracy, family meaning, luxury | deep dress-code knowledge, fabric quality, embroidery, ceremony. No shortcuts |
| Contemporary and fusion | heritage materials or motifs in modern silhouettes | hardest to do well. Respect the source, add something new, do not just mix |
| Luxury and export | material quality, global positioning, provenance | Pashmina, Allo, Dhaka as luxury with documented origin and artisan story |
| Sustainable and ethical | fair trade, natural materials, low impact, transparency | Nepal's handloom is inherently sustainable, lead with it and document it |
| Uniforms and workwear | function, durability, identity, comfort, budget | practical fabric, fit across bodies, clear branding |
| Accessories | bags, scarves, topis, jewelry, belts | smaller scope, same rigor, often the entry point for a new brand |

## Fashion marketing and presentation

**Online:** lookbook, e-commerce product pages (flat lay, on-model, detail, plus fabric, sizing, care, story), social content (reels, carousels, behind-the-scenes, calendar), campaign creative, brand story and about page, influencer and styling briefs, email (launches, styling guides, seasonal drops).

**Offline:** line sheet (product, price, fabric, sizing per style), tech pack (flat, measurements, materials, construction, colorways), swing tags and labels (brand, care, fiber, origin, fair-trade cert), printed lookbook, exhibition and trade-show materials, packaging and the unboxing experience.

Pull in the right crew: Copywriter for product copy, Videographer and Cinematographer for lookbook film, Concept artist for moodboards, Brand for positioning, Pricing for wholesale and retail, Content for social and campaigns.
